MINA is the capstone for Michigan State University's Institute for Digital Archaeology Practice and Methods. MINA offers 'proof-of-concept' for a Web-based platform that facilitates deeper insights into Indian archaeology. It is premised on the belief that the present state of things influences our knowledge and interpretation of the past. By making this relationship between present and past explicit, we can reframe current understandings and promote new empirical research in Indian archaeology.

WHY?

MINA draws attention to the isssue of disciplinary silos, and begins to address these concerns in the practice of Indian archaeology. Specifically,

(1) the challenges in developing digital methodologies for post-colonial societies;

(2) the oversight in development of appropriate digital and technologies for archaeology, particularly Web-based visualization

HOW?

MINA brings together Web-mapping tools, geovisualization and published archaeology reports. A key source is Indian Archaeology - a Review (IAR) compliled by the Archaeological Survey of India (or Survey), the national department for archaeology and hertiage management in India. IAR offers edited summaries of annual reports that Indian archaeologists have submitted to the Survey, as part of a project's excavation license. Therefore, IAR documents field collection throughout India for every twelve-month period.

The archaeological community in India consists of three broadly conceptualized knowledge producers: the national department (Survey), university and research institutions and state departments of archaeology.
